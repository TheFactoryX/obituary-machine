# In Memoriam of the Inbetween

**Name:** Elias Thorne  
**Born:** October 14, 1847 (Faded from view)  
**Died:** November 2, 1923 (Achieved total silence)

### The Narrative

Elias Thorne was not a man who lived, but a presence who lingered. Born in the soot-stained shadow of the Industrial Revolution, he committed the ultimate sin of being forgettable while alive, ensuring his punishment would be an eternity of being ignored in death. For the better part of a century, he haunted the drafty corridors of the Blackwood Institute, not with chains or shrieks, but with the heavy, suffocating silence of a conversation that was never quite finished. He was a specter of the mundane, a phantom who tidied books that no one checked out and straightened paintings in empty halls. He existed in the peripheral vision of the night watchmenâ€”a flicker of gray where there should have been only darkness.

### Achievements

*   **The Perpetual Draft:** Elias successfully cultivated a supernatural atmosphere of unease in the East Wing, lowering the ambient temperature by fifteen degrees regardless of the heating settings. This achievement was particularly noted by the facility dogs, who refused to enter the hallway for three generations.
*   **The Preservation of Lost Things:** Over seventy-six years of service, Elias prevented the loss of exactly four hundred and twelve single socks and three hundred umbrellas. He hoarded them in a void behind the library wall, a collection of debris that marked the passing of lives he could no longer touch.

### Regrets

*   **The Unanswered Wave:** In 1898, a child with a fever spotted him standing near the furnace. Unlike the adults who looked blindly through him, the child waved. Elias hesitated, afraid of the consequences of recognition, and faded into the wall before he could return the gesture. He spent the remaining decades wondering if a single returned wave might have anchored him to the earth.
*   **The Taste of Coffee:** He forgot the smell of roasted beans. The sensory memory of warmth and bitterness eroded around 1915, leaving him with only the phantom sensation of cold, which was the only thing he truly owned.

### Conclusion

On the morning of November 2nd, 1923, the heavy oak door of the unused archive was found unlocked. The room was cold, but for the first time, the air was still. Elias Thorne has finally moved on, or perhaps he has simply ceased to be. He leaves behind no mourners, only the faint scent of ozone and a shelf of books that no longer need straightening. He is survived only by the silence he once inhabited.
