# The Unseen Departure

**Name:** Elias Thorne
**Born:** October 14, 1892
**Died:** November 3, 1924
**Location:** The Stairwell of the Blackwood Manor

***

### A Life in Passing
Elias Thorne was a man of quiet habits and softer footfalls, a soul who seemingly evaporated from the mortal coil the very moment his heart ceased to beat. Born to the smog-choked air of the industrial revolution, he spent his thirty-two years in the service of silence, moving through the world as if he were already rehearsing for his final audition. He was a man who loved rain because it allowed him to walk the streets without the burden of greeting his neighbors, and he loved the dark because it offered the anonymity he desperately craved. In the end, he died as he lived: alone in a dusty room, waiting for a change in the weather that never came. Now, he haunts the hallways not with malice, but with the heavy, static sorrow of a song left unfinished.

### Notable Disturbances
**Master of the Unseen Draft**
Throughout his spectral residency, Elias perfected the art of the thermal anomaly. He became the sudden chill in a sunlit room, the inexplicable gust of wind that slams a shut window, and the prickle of skin on the back of a neck. He achieved a temperature drop that could shatter a thermometer, proving that cold is not merely the absence of heat, but the presence of a lonely history.

**The Weaver of Whisper**
While incapable of articulate speech, Elias managed to manipulate the acoustic architecture of the home. He learned to transmute the settling of the house into the cadence of a human voice. He is credited with the auditory phenomenon of hearing one’s name called in an empty basement—a melancholy echo designed solely to remind the living that they, too, are temporary.

### Lingering Remorse
**The Unsent Letter**
In life, Elias harbored a profound, paralyzing affection for a woman who lived three doors down. In death, his greatest torment is the letter he wrote but never mailed, which remains yellowing and unread in the floorboards beneath the attic. His endless pacing is not meant to frighten; it is the desperate, useless march of a man trying to reach the mailbox before the dawn.

**The Fear of Being Forgotten**
He regrets not making more noise when he had a tongue. Now that he is reduced to knocking on pipes and flickering lights, he realizes the terrible irony of his existence: he spent his life wanting to disappear, and now he spends his eternity terrified that he finally has.
